**Subtitle: Exploiting the flaws of a broken system without mistaking the system for justice**

## **1. Meritocracy as an Organizational Myth**

In the modern corporate lexicon, **meritocracy** is revered as a sacred ideal. The principle is simple and morally appealing: talent and effort should determine reward. It promises a world where the competent rise, the lazy fall, and justice appears through performance metrics.

But actual organizations do not operate in that clean world.

Meritocracy is useful as an aspiration, but dangerous as a description. It encourages people to believe that outcomes are pure reflections of ability, when in reality they are filtered through timing, visibility, politics, resource constraints, manager preference, and organizational convenience.

The ideal equation is:

$$
\text{Reward} = f(\text{Talent},\ \text{Effort})
$$

The organizational equation is closer to:

$$
\operatorname{Reward}_i
\approx
f(C_i,\ E_i,\ V_i,\ A_i,\ T_i,\ P_i)
$$

where:

$$
\begin{aligned}
C_i &: \text{competence} \\
E_i &: \text{effort} \\
V_i &: \text{visibility} \\
A_i &: \text{availability} \\
T_i &: \text{timing} \\
P_i &: \text{political and strategic position}
\end{aligned}
$$

The uncomfortable truth is that **competence is necessary but not sufficient**. Organizations reward what they can see, use, trust, and deploy at the moment a need appears.

## **2. The Double-Edged Sword of Pure Merit**

A purist version of meritocracy can easily produce a Hobbesian internal order: a "*war of all against all*." When every promotion, bonus, and reputation marker is interpreted as proof of relative worth, cooperation starts to decay.

The goal shifts from building durable value to winning visible comparison.

| Meritocratic Pressure | Organizational Distortion |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Constant ranking** | Employees optimize for comparison rather than mission |
| **Short-cycle metrics** | Long-term work is sacrificed for immediate proof |
| **Winner-take-most rewards** | Collaboration becomes politically risky |
| **Public performance narratives** | People defend image rather than learn |
| **Permanent self-justification** | Burnout becomes a hidden tax |

The alternative, however, is not attractive either. A seniority-based gerontocracy creates its own failure mode: complacency, blocked mobility, and the exodus of high-performers who refuse to wait quietly while lower-energy incumbents occupy scarce opportunity.

The management problem is therefore not meritocracy versus seniority. It is **calibration**.

$$
\text{Healthy Allocation}
\approx
\alpha \cdot \text{Merit}
+
(1-\alpha) \cdot \text{Stability}
$$

The hard part is choosing \(\alpha\) under uncertainty.

## **3. The Leadership Calibration Problem**

The tension becomes sharpest in leadership appointments. How much patience should an organization give a faltering new leader?

A **zero-tolerance** policy looks decisive, but it often damages the leadership pipeline. If every difficult leadership role becomes a trial by fire with no learning curve, capable people stop volunteering for risky mandates. Demoted leaders rarely return to ordinary execution with perfect humility; more often, they leave, disengage, or quietly poison the team climate.

But infinite patience is equally dangerous. An incompetent or toxic leader does not merely underperform alone. They suppress the productivity of the team beneath them, block the emergence of new talent, and often trigger resignations among the most capable subordinates.

The leadership decision can be framed as a trade-off:

| Decision | Immediate Advantage | Hidden Cost |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Remove early** | Limits visible damage | Weakens leadership experimentation |
| **Wait longer** | Preserves continuity | Lets dysfunction compound |
| **Reassign carefully** | Saves capability where possible | Requires political and emotional skill |
| **Ignore the problem** | Avoids conflict | Converts one weak leader into a system-wide liability |

The core question is not whether the person is struggling. Many leaders struggle early. The question is whether the struggle is a **learning curve** or a **trust collapse**.

Learning curves can be coached. Trust collapses spread.

## **4. Available Inventory Beats Abstract Preparedness**

Because HR systems are imperfect, opportunity often follows a logic of logistical convenience. Organizations use the people who are visible, available, and trusted enough right now.

This is the logic of **available inventory**.

The abstractly prepared candidate is not always selected. The concretely available candidate often is.

$$
\Pr(\operatorname{Opportunity}_i)
\uparrow
\quad \text{when} \quad
\operatorname{Available}_i
\land
\operatorname{Visible}_i
\land
\operatorname{Trusted}_i
$$

This is not always corruption. Often it is bounded rationality. Managers operate under time pressure, incomplete information, and execution risk. They do not search for the theoretical optimum; they search for someone who can plausibly carry the problem now.

That creates a painful asymmetry:

| Person | Capability | Position | Likely Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High competence, low visibility** | Strong | Peripheral | Underused or overlooked |
| **Moderate competence, high visibility** | Adequate | Central | Repeatedly considered |
| **High competence, high trust** | Strong | Deployable | Becomes strategic inventory |
| **Low competence, high visibility** | Fragile | Exposed | Receives opportunity, then risks collapse |

The system does not merely ask, "Who is best?" It asks, "Who can be used?"

## **5. The Geopolitics of Positioning**

The professional implication is cold but useful: skill accumulation is not enough. One must also practice the **geopolitics of positioning**.

This means reading the organization as a map of resource flows, attention, bottlenecks, and strategic chokepoints. The goal is not to become hollowly political. The goal is to place real competence where it can be recognized and converted into leverage.

The guiding question is:

> Where should my capability stand so that it becomes useful when the organization turns its attention?

Several signals matter:

| Signal | What It Reveals |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Budget movement** | Where the company is willing to spend |
| **Executive repetition** | Which problems are becoming strategic narratives |
| **Operational bottlenecks** | Where solving one interface unlocks many others |
| **Talent scarcity** | Which capabilities are difficult to replace |
| **Visibility gradient** | Which work creates legible proof rather than invisible exhaustion |

The person stationed on the periphery can be excellent and still remain unseen. The person standing at a chokepoint may be merely good, yet become indispensable.

This is not an argument against competence. It is an argument against passive competence.

## **6. Exploiting the System Without Becoming Hollow**

The phrase "exploiting the system" can sound cynical, but there is an important distinction.

Bad exploitation means gaming appearances while neglecting substance. Strategic positioning means making substance legible.

| Hollow Politics | Strategic Positioning |
| :--- | :--- |
| Performs importance | Solves visible constraints |
| Seeks proximity without contribution | Moves toward leverage with capability |
| Uses ambiguity to inflate status | Uses clarity to demonstrate value |
| Avoids accountability | Accepts measurable ownership |
| Converts attention into image | Converts attention into output |

The goal is not to trick the system into rewarding emptiness. The goal is to avoid being naively trapped by the system's blindness.

In a flawed meritocracy, the disciplined professional must do two things at once:

$$
\text{Career Leverage}
\approx
\text{Capability}
\times
\text{Positioning}
$$

If capability is near zero, positioning becomes exposure. If positioning is near zero, capability becomes invisibility.

## **7. Conclusion**

Meritocracy is not false because talent and effort do not matter. It is false because talent and effort are not the only things that matter.

Organizations are imperfect allocation machines. They reward the visible, the available, the trusted, the timely, and the strategically placed. The prize does not always go to the absolute best person. It often goes to the competent person standing closest to the problem when the spotlight turns.

The practical lesson is not to abandon merit, but to stop worshiping it as an automatic mechanism.

**Build capability. Move toward resource flow. Occupy chokepoints. Make value legible.** In a broken meritocracy, excellence must not merely exist; it must be positioned.
